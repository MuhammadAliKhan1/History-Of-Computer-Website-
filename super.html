CDC 6600 Supercomputer
(1964)

Outperforming IBM 7030 Stretch

The CDC 6600 was the flagship of the 6000 series of mainframe computer systems manufactured by Control Data Corporation. Generally considered to be the first successful supercomputer, The Control Data Corporation (CDC) 6600 performs up to 3 million instructions per second —three times faster than that of its closest competitor, the IBM 7030 supercomputer. The 6600 retained the distinction of being the fastest computer in the world until surpassed by its successor, the CDC 7600, in 1968.

Insight Of CDC 6600 Supercomputer

The CDC 6600 used a simplified core processor that was designed to run mathematical and logic operations as rapidly as possible, which demanded it was built as small as possible to reduce the length of wiring and the associated signalling delays. This led to the machine's (typically) cross-shaped main chassis with the circuit boards for the CPU arranged close to the center. Supporting the CPU were ten channel controllers that handled input and output, as well as controlling what data was sent into the main CPU for processing, a concept known as "barrel and slot". The 6600 used a 60-bit word and a ones'-complement representation of integers, something that later CDC machines would use into the late 1980s, making them the last systems to use this architecture.
